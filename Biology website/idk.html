The different circulatory systems

The systems

Every system has its place in nature. Different organisms use different systems. These are ways that blood is exchanged with the rest of the organism

System Function Illustration
Diffusion This occurs when the organism is very small or very thin, down to one cell. At this scale, every cell in the organism can obtain vital nutrients and gas independently through the membrane. The issue with using diffusion is that the organism needs to be moist and surrounded by the needed components. Diffusion
Closed A closed circulatory system is a system which is seperated from the rest of the bodly fluids. This makes exchange of gas and nutrients very effective because it goes through closed canals
Open An open circulatory system mixes fluids and organs together. It contains one or more hearts with bloodveins that ends into a pool that leads the blood back to the heart. The blood is called hemoglyph. The hemoglyph enters the body between contractions and surrounds the cells allowind gas and nutrients to exchange.

The heart

Chambers Function Illustration
Double-chambered This means that the heart or hearts contain two chambers. This is often to differentiate the intake and outtake of blood from the heart. Double chambered hearts are common in singular system organisms. This solution requires less energy and organisms that are active in short periods of time, can benefit from this. However, the disatvantage is the low pressure that the two chambers can produce. The organisms that uses this heart structure can't sustain high activity levels for a long period of time.
Three-chambered A three-chambered heart has two separate chambers that comes from the lungs and body. As the blood enters the heart, it is mixed and sent onwards to lungs and body from one chamber. This results in the blood mixing, which can lead to inefficient transport of gas and nutrients. The three-chambered heart requires a bit more energy than a two-chambered heart, but it is also capable of pumping blood more efficiently and with more pressure.
Four-chambered Four-chambered hearts are often found in humans and other primates. They provide seperate chambers for blood that is going to the respiratory system and blood that is being pumped out to the body. It also separates the blood coming in from the body and the blood coming from the lungs. Four chambers give optimal blood transport with higher pressure and better separation of the blood.

The different respiratory systems

System Function Illustration
Diffusion Gas moves in and out of cells through the membrane Diffusion
Gills Bloodveins going against the current pick up oksygen from the water and remove carbondioxide from the blood. Gills are located on the outside of the fish because they are always in contact with water.
Lungs Air flows through thin pipes surrounded by bloodveins in a muscle that contracts and extends. The oxygen moves to the blood and carbondioxide moves into the lung. Every breath oxygen comes in and carbondioxide gets removed. The lungs are inside the body because otherwise they would dry out and stop working.
Air sacks Birds have two air sacks that contract and extend to remove and take in air. When a bird breathes the air goes into one sack. Then it contracts and sends the air through a set of thin pipes with bloodveins around. The oxygen goes to the blood with diffusion and carbondioxide goes out from the blood with the same prosess. The air then gets sucked into the second air sack and then out. This process allows for constant oxygen in the body and is much more effective than normal lungs.

The excretory and osmoregulation systems

The excretory systems

Excretory systems are mainly to dispose of nitrogen waste. This is done with three different waste mediums (listed below), each with their pros and cons.

Medium Poisonousness Solubility
Ammonia (NH3) Very Water
Urea Somewhat Water
Uric Acid Not Fat

A non-poison medium helps not kill children if they are laid in eggs on land, but ammonia is easier to produce.

To get rid of these mediums are a variety of systems. They, alog with the medium they use, are listed below.

Waste management in the body serves the purposes of osmoregulation and excretion.

System Description Medium
Diffusion Ammonia goes straight through the membrane and gets lost in the universe forever. NH3
Proto-nephridium Often combined with diffusion, protonephridia are a primitive form of kidneys. They excrete through several openings on the surface of its organism. Found in flatworms. NH3
Meta-nephridium The posh cousin of protonephridia, the metanephridium thinks it's all the rage just because it's connected to the blood stream. They excrete through a pipe in each segment of its organism. Found in annelids. NH3
Malphigian tubes The malphagian tubes are found in in some insects, arachnids, myriapods, and tardigrades. It absorbs waste from the hemolymph on its way out through diffusion and pumpos, and send it out to the surface of the body. Uric Acid
Gills Found in most fish, gills provide both respiration and excretion. Ammonia can diffuse through the thin membranes. NH3
Kidneys The state of the art in osmoregulation, kidneys rinse the blood and ensure everything's in balance. Found in vertabrates. Uric Acid for birds and reptiles, mostly urea for mammals.

The systems of osmoregulation

Osmoregulation is management of the osmotic pressure, to manage fluid and salt balance.

System Description Medium
Diffusion Ammonia goes through the membrane and gets lost in the universe forever. NH3
Isoosmosic For saltwater creatures, keeping their salt balance in check is easy. Diffusion takes care of it for them. NH3
Proto-nephridium Often combined with diffusion, protonephridia are a primitive form of kidneys. They excrete through several openings on the surface of its organism. Found in flatworms. NH3

Gills Gills also provide osmoregulation. In freshwater fish they let water come in to lessen the salt percentage, and in saltwater they let water get out to heighten the salt percentage. NH3
Kidneys The state of the art in excretion, kidneys rinse the blood and ensure everything's in balance. Found in vertabrates. Uric Acid for birds and reptiles, urea for mammals.

The reproductive systems

Reproduction is a way for an organism to further its genes by producing new organisms

Reproduction with genders

System Description
Asexual reproduction Asexual reproduction is formation which is not bound by gender. Mitosis is an example of asexual reproduction. The cell can split in two parts or more to form copies of itself who can live separatly. Found in small organisms and rarely animals.
Hermaphrodite A hermaphrodite organism is an organism who is able to produce both gendercells within itself without help from the outside.
Gendered reproduction Gendered reproduction, commonly knows as the reproduction system that humans use, involves a male gendercell and a female gendercell which formes a new organism. This usually requires two organisms of the same species unless the organism is hermaphrodite. In gendered reproduction, one of the organisms involved often keep both cells to reproduce. Some organisms put these two cells in an egg.

Eggs

Shell Description
Soft-shell Soft-shell eggs require moist and wet surroundings to survive, this is due to the thin structure of the shell. The thin and elastic egg can cause drainage of water if the egg was in a dry area. Organisms that lay soft-shelled eggs often live in water or very humid areas.
Hard-shell Hard-shell eggs have an extra layer of hard mineral outside of the soft-shell. These eggs often contain water to protect and nuture the fetus. Since these eggs are hard, they can be kept on land and not dry out. Hard-shelled eggs are often found in organisms that live on land.
